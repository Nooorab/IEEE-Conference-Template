\section{Introduction} % ~ 1000 words Monia
PDF files are widely used for public communication by Swedish government agencies and institutions. Their popularity comes from their ability to preserve document layout and maintain compatibility across different systems, making them a reliable format for publishing official materials such as reports, policy documents, guidelines, and administrative records \cite{iso32000}. In Sweden, this reliance on digital publication is closely connected to the broader transition toward digital governance and the principle of public access to official records, Offentlighetsprincipen, which gives the public and media the right to insight into the activities of public authorities \cite{swe_public_access}. This principle supports democratic transparency and accountability \cite{swe_public_access}. However, because large volumes of official documents are made publicly available, publication workflows also become part of the public sector’s security posture. If documents are not reviewed or sanitised before publication, they may expose information that was never intended for public access [Add source here].

Beyond the content visible to readers, PDF files often contain embedded metadata describing how the file was created, edited, converted, and processed \cite{spiegel2017}. This metadata may include author names, usernames, software names and versions, producer fields, and traces of internal workflows \cite{spiegel2017}. Although this information is usually not visible to ordinary readers, it can be extracted using common forensic and metadata-analysis tools [Add source here]. For public-sector organisations, this makes metadata hygiene an important but sometimes overlooked part of information security. Metadata hygiene refers to the practice of reviewing, minimising, or removing unnecessary embedded information before documents are published [Add source here]. In a public-sector publishing context, exposed metadata may indicate that document review and sanitisation routines are not applied consistently before publication. Poor metadata hygiene can therefore be viewed as a sign of gaps in organisational information security practices, rather than as an inherent flaw in the PDF format itself.

From a digital forensics perspective, metadata is valuable because it can provide insight into a document’s origin, creation process, and later modifications \cite{spiegel2017, [Add source here]}. At the same time, when this information is exposed unintentionally, it may create security risks by revealing details about internal systems, user environments, naming conventions, or organisational structures [Add source here]. These details may seem minor on their own, but when collected across many documents, they can support organisational fingerprinting by helping attackers build a broader picture of an organisation’s technical environment, software dependencies, document-management practices, and working routines. In this way, exposed metadata can expand the attack surface during reconnaissance. For example, staff names or usernames may support spear-phishing attempts, while software and version information may help attackers identify specific tools or possible weaknesses to target [Add source here]. Metadata leakage can therefore support more targeted attacks, even when the exposed information appears harmless on its own. Previous research has shown that metadata leakage can support infrastructure fingerprinting and targeted cyber attacks by providing contextual information about both individuals and systems \cite{adhatarao2021}, \cite{hasegawa2022}.

The risks are not limited to ordinary metadata fields. A central feature of the PDF format is its support for incremental updates \cite{iso32000}. When a PDF is modified, the file is not always rewritten from scratch. Instead, new or changed objects may be appended to the existing file, together with updated cross-reference information and trailer data \cite{iso32000}. This design can preserve earlier document states, meaning that deleted, overwritten, or finalised content may remain recoverable unless the document has been properly sanitised, flattened, or regenerated [Add source here]. As a result, a publicly available PDF may contain more than its visible final version. It may also include structural traces of earlier edits, hidden content, or previous versions of objects that were assumed to have been removed \cite{adhatarao2021}.

Despite growing awareness of metadata-related risks, limited empirical research has focused specifically on metadata exposure in Swedish public-sector documents. This gap is especially relevant in a highly digitalised environment such as Sweden, where public organisations increasingly rely on digital communication and where regulatory frameworks such as the NIS2 Directive place greater emphasis on cyber security, risk management, and organisational accountability [Add source here]. Understanding how metadata is exposed in publicly available documents is therefore important not only from a forensic perspective, but also from the perspective of organisational security hygiene and regulatory readiness.

\subsection{Research Problem}
The research problem is that publicly available PDFs may contain hidden metadata and structural traces that expose sensitive organisational information, but little is known about the scale, severity, and patterns of this exposure in the Swedish public sector. This study therefore investigates whether publicly available PDFs from Swedish public-sector organisations leak metadata or structural traces that may reveal information about internal systems, software environments, user patterns, document workflows, and organisational structures.

This exposure is significant because such information may support organisational fingerprinting when collected across many documents. By combining exposed metadata from multiple files, attackers may build a broader picture of an organisation’s technical environment and working routines. This can support reconnaissance, spear-phishing attempts, and other targeted attacks.

In addition, metadata exposure reflects broader questions of organisational security hygiene. Public-sector organisations often publish large numbers of documents through different systems and workflows, which increases the risk that embedded information is preserved unintentionally. Understanding how often such exposure occurs, what types of information are leaked, and how severe the exposure is can therefore provide insight into both cybersecurity risks and digital forensic implications.

\subsection{Research Question}
This study is guided by the following main research question:

To what extent, and with what severity, do publicly available PDF documents from Swedish public-sector organisations expose sensitive information through metadata and structural content leakage?

The following sub-questions support the main research question:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1}: How does the prevalence and severity of metadata exposure vary across institutional tiers?
    \item \textbf{RQ2}: How do metadata exposure patterns change across the pre-NIS2, NIS2 preparation, and post-NIS2 implementation periods?
    \item \textbf{RQ3}: To what extent is unintended information recoverable from the incremental update history of publicly available Swedish public-sector PDFs?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scope and Delimitations}
This study addresses this gap by analysing metadata and hidden version history in publicly available PDF documents from Swedish public-sector organisations. The analysis examines PDF documents collected from 30 Swedish government agencies and institutions in order to identify patterns of information exposure, assess what metadata reveals about internal systems and workflows, and evaluate the implications from both cybersecurity and digital forensic perspectives.

The scope of the study is limited to documents that were publicly available through official websites and online archives at the time of collection. Unpublished documents and files stored in internal systems that are not publicly accessible are therefore excluded. The study also focuses specifically on PDF metadata and structural content leakage, rather than the visible textual content of the documents or the broader cybersecurity posture of each organisation.


\section{Related Work} % ~ 600 words Monia
Research on embedded metadata has shown that publicly available documents can reveal information beyond their visible content. Metadata may include details about document authorship, software use, and editing history. While these details can be useful in digital forensics, they may also create security risks when exposed unintentionally. Several previous studies have examined this issue in public-sector and government-related contexts, showing that metadata leakage may support organisational fingerprinting, reconnaissance, and targeted attacks.

One relevant study is Mendelman’s work on metadata exposure in Estonian governmental organisations. The study examined publicly available documents from government websites and found that metadata could reveal information about internal users, software environments, and organisational practices \cite{mendelman2018}. This suggests how public documents can become a source of intelligence about an organisation, even when their visible content is not considered sensitive. Mendelman’s work is therefore closely related to the present study, as both examine how metadata in public-sector documents may expose internal organisational information.

Research from the Netherlands has examined similar issues. Mulder et al. investigated metadata leakage in documents from Dutch public organisations and found that they could contain traces of document creation, editing, and internal workflows \cite{mulder2017}. Their work is relevant because it places metadata leakage within a European public-sector context, where digital administration and transparency are central. However, Sweden differs from this context, particularly because of Offentlighetsprincipen, which creates a strong legal and cultural expectation that official documents should be accessible to the public.

Adhatarao and Lauradoux provide a more technical perspective by studying hidden data in PDF files published by security agencies across several countries \cite{adhatarao2021}. Their work is particularly relevant because it focuses on PDFs as a file format and the types of information that may remain embedded after publication. They show that PDFs can expose author information, software details, system-related traces, and other metadata that may be useful for attackers. Their study also discusses sanitisation and highlights that removing hidden data from PDFs is not always straightforward. This is important for the present study because it shows that metadata hygiene is not only about whether metadata is present, but also about whether organisations have effective routines for detecting and removing it before documents are published.

Hasegawa, Saito, and Sasaki further developed this area by analysing metadata in PDF files published by police agencies in Japan \cite{hasegawa2022}. Their research is relevant because police agencies are public-sector organisations where information security is expected to be especially important. The fact that metadata exposure can still occur in this context suggests that the issue is not limited to organisations with weak security awareness. Instead, it may reflect broader challenges in managing document workflows, metadata removal, and publication routines. Their research also provides a useful comparison point for the present study, as both examine metadata exposure in public-sector PDFs.

Together, these studies show that metadata leakage in public documents is a recognised security and forensic issue. Previous research has found that exposed metadata can reveal user information, software environments, document workflows, and hidden structural content. It can also support organisational fingerprinting by allowing outsiders to build a picture of how an organisation works internally. However, existing research has mainly focused on Estonia, the Netherlands, international security agencies, and Japanese police organisations. There is still limited empirical work on metadata exposure in Swedish public-sector PDFs. This study therefore builds on earlier research by examining whether publicly available PDFs from Swedish public-sector organisations expose metadata or structural traces, and by analysing the extent and severity of that exposure across institutions and time periods.

\section{Methodology} % ~ 1200 words Kajsa
This study employs a mixed-methods design, combining quantitative measurement with qualitative forensic analysis. The quantitative component establishes the prevalence and severity of metadata leakage at scale, enabling statistical comparison across institutional tiers and temporal strata. The qualitative component examines the specific content recoverable from incremental update layers, providing interpretive depth that prevalence rates alone cannot capture. 

\subsection{Data Collection}
\subsubsection{Exposure Categories}
This study identifies two structurally distinct categories of unintended information exposure in PDF documents. The first is metadata field leakage; structured key-value attributes embedded in the document's Info Dictionary or XMP stream that reveal information about authorship, software, and infrastructure [KÄLLA]. The second is structural content leakage; the preservation of superseded document versions through the PDF incremental update mechanism [KÄLLA]. While both categories contribute to the overall attack surface, they differ in nature. While metadata field leakage primarily enables reconnaissance, structural content leakage may expose information the author explicitly intended to remove [KÄLLA]. These two categories are assessed separately throughout this study.

\subsubsection{Sampling Strategy}
This study employs a two-stage purposive stratified sampling design, operating at both the institutional and document level.

\paragraph{Institutional Sampling (Stage 1)}
At the first stage, 30 Swedish public-sector authorities and institutions were selected through purposive sampling. Rather than random selection, entities were chosen to ensure representation across institutional function, security mandate, and organizational scale. The selected entities were assigned to one of three tiers according to the operational sensitivity of their mandate:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Tier 1: Strategic \& Sovereign Risk:} Entities whose primary function concerns national security, intelligence, law enforcement, or executive governance. 
    \item \textbf{Tier 2: Systemic \& Structural Risk:} Entities responsible for critical national infrastructure, financial oversight, and digital governance. 
    \item \textbf{Tier 3: Societal \& Privacy Risk:} Entities that process large volumes of sensitive personal or public-interest data, including healthcare authorities, universities, and social insurance bodies. For a complete list of entities by tier, see Appendix A.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Document Sampling (Stage 2)}
At the second stage, documents were collected from each authority using a quota-based sampling frame. 

\paragraph{Temporal Stratification}
The dataset was stratified temporally into three periods aligned with the legislative trajectory of the EU NIS2 Directive \cite{eu-nis2}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pre-NIS2 (2020–2023):} Baseline period prior to the adoption of the NIS2 Directive.
    \item \textbf{NIS2 Preparation (2024–2025):} Transposition period during which member states were required to implement the directive into national law.
    \item \textbf{Post-NIS2 Implementation (2026–present):} Period following the entry into force of Sweden's national implementation of NIS2 as `Cybersäkerhetslagen'.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Collection Pipeline}
A custom automated pipeline was constructed for document discovery, acquisition, and forensic processing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Language:} Python 3.x
    \item \textbf{Libraries:} requests, BeautifulSoup4, hashlib
    \item \textbf{Forensic Tool:} ExifTool (v12.x)
    \item \textbf{Identification:} SHA-256
    \item \textbf{Total Scope:} $\sim$3,000 PDFs 
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Automated Discovery}
The primary collection tool was a Python-based crawler using BeautifulSoup4 for HTML parsing. The crawler enforced a stay-within-domain policy to ensure data was only collected from targeted servers. Navigation prioritized high-value publication directories, including /publikationer, /rapporter, and /arkiv. For authorities where the main crawler was blocked by WAF infrastructure (Radware or Cloudflare) or where documents were only accessible via JavaScript-rendered search interfaces, dedicated focused scrapers were constructed using direct URL injection or Playwright-based pagination.

\subsubsection{Acquisition and Filtering}
Documents were collected according to a quota-based sampling frame to ensure balanced representation across the targeted time periods. A quota of 50 documents per authority and temporal stratum was enforced to ensure balanced representation across both institutions and time periods, and to prevent high-volume publishers from dominating the dataset. Where an authority did not produce sufficient documents within a given stratum, all available documents were collected without enforcing the quota. Publication year was determined by evaluating, in order of priority: the URL path, the HTTP Last-Modified response header, and the XMP:CreateDate internal metadata field.

\subsubsection{Processing and Integrity}
To maintain a verifiable chain of custody, each acquired file underwent immediate forensic processing upon download. A SHA-256 hash was computed and recorded for every document, serving as a unique identifier and ensuring that all subsequent analysis was performed on the exact byte-sequence as collected. Duplicate files, identified by a matching hash, were excluded from analysis to prevent double-counting.

Metadata extraction was performed using ExifTool (v12.x), a widely adopted command-line tool validated in prior digital forensics literature [KÄLLA]. ExifTool was invoked per-file in JSON output mode, enabling the extraction of all available metadata fields regardless of schema. The resulting output was normalized and stored in a relational long-format CSV structure, where each row represents a single field–value pair linked to the file's SHA-256 hash, authority, and collection period. This schema enables systematic cross-authority and cross-field comparison.

In parallel, the binary structure of each PDF was analyzed to detect the presence of incremental update layers. Detection was performed by counting occurrences of the \texttt{\%\%EOF} end-of-file marker in the raw byte stream, as each appended revision layer introduces a new cross-reference table and a corresponding \texttt{\%\%EOF} marker. To eliminate false positives, the linearization status of each document was evaluated prior to classification: a linearized PDF inherently contains two \texttt{\%\%EOF} markers as part of its standard structure, and was therefore only flagged if the marker count exceeded two. Non-linearized documents were flagged at a count greater than one. Documents satisfying these criteria were tagged for further forensic examination. 

\subsection{Analytical Framework}

\subsubsection{Metadata Classification}
In this study, document metadata is treated as a passive information exposure factor. To systematically classify the confidentiality risk posed by each identified metadata field, this study applies the R0–R3 risk taxonomy. The taxonomy is grounded in the risk assessment principles established by NIST SP 800-60 and FIPS 199. NIST SP 800-60 provides a framework for mapping information types to security impact levels based on the potential consequences of unauthorized disclosure, while FIPS 199 defines the three security objectives (confidentiality, integrity, and availability) and establishes Low, Moderate, and High as the standardized impact categories [KÄLLA]. The R0–R3 taxonomy operationalizes these impact levels in a four-point scale adapted specifically for metadata field classification, where each level reflects the degree to which a disclosed field could enable identification, targeting, or infrastructure mapping by an adversary. The following categories were applied:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R0 (Non-Sensitive):} Benign structural parameters. File dimensions, PDF version numbers, page counts.
    \item \textbf{R1 (Low Risk):} Broad organizational identities and generic timestamps. Department names, generalized software tools and creation dates without specific timezone mapping.
    \item \textbf{R2 (Medium Risk):} Explicit attribution identifiers mapping to specific internal entities or highly particularized configurations. Specific author names, internal document management IDs, exact patch levels of software, customized printing or pre-press work-flow flags.
    \item \textbf{R3 (High Risk):} Data elements directly charting internal network topographies or system schemas. Absolute file pathways mapped to local C: drives, mapped network shares, internal server IP schemas, organizational Active Directory usernames, or hidden architectural tokens.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Structural content leakage} 
To evaluate the extent to which sensitive information can be recovered from document histories, we utilized a qualitative forensic audit focused on "Revision Bloat" and substantive content restoration. Recovery success was measured through a three-stage filter: technical presence of incremental saves, forensic accessibility, and verifiably recovered text alterations (unique ADDED/REMOVED string pairs). To analyze the depth of the exposure, we identified "outlier" documents exhibiting extreme revision bloat, defined as documents harboring more than 50 hidden versions. These cases were analyzed individually to identify high-value data leaks, providing a qualitative assessment of the "worst-case" information exposure.

\subsection{Statistical Framework}

\subsubsection{Tier Comparison}
To quantitatively evaluate the disparity in metadata hygiene across the Swedish administrative landscape, we employed Pearson’s Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) tests of independence. The statistical matrix was constructed as a $3 \times 2$ contingency table, mapping the three organizational Tiers (Independent Variable) against a binary risk classification (Dependent Variable).

For the purpose of statistical validity, we established a "Significant Risk" threshold by aggregating R2 and R3 occurrences. This aggregation ensured that all cells within the contingency matrix maintained an expected frequency of at least 5, satisfying the fundamental assumptions of the Chi-Square distribution.

The null hypothesis posited that metadata leakage rates are independent of an organization's Tier, while the alternative hypothesis asserted a systemic correlation between organizational mission and digital hygiene. All calculations were performed using Python's \texttt{scipy.stats} library, with the significance threshold set at $p < 0.05$.

\subsubsection{Temporal Comparison}
To evaluate the chronological evolution of metadata hygiene relative to the enactment of the Cybersäkerhetslagen, we executed a longitudinal Pearson’s Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) test of independence. The statistical matrix was constructed as a $3 \times 2$ contingency table, mapping the three distinct temporal epochs against the binary R2+ risk classification.

Consistent with our sectoral analysis, we utilized the aggregated "Significant Risk" (R2+) threshold to satisfy the Chi-Square requirement for expected cell frequencies ($E \geq 5$). The null hypothesis posited that metadata exposure rates are independent of the document publication period, indicating a lack of programmatic response to legislative pressure. Conversely, the alternative hypothesis asserted that document hygiene improved significantly in correlation with the Swedish legislative lifecycle. Significance was evaluated at the $p < 0.05$ threshold.

\section{Ethical Considerations} % ~ 200 words Monia 
This study only examines PDF documents that were publicly available through official websites and online archives at the time of collection. In Sweden, this relates to the principle of public access to information, Offentlighetsprincipen, which supports transparency and access to official documents held by public authorities \cite{swe_public_access}. However, public availability does not remove the responsibility to handle the data carefully, especially when metadata may contain names, usernames, software details, workflow traces, or other organisational and technical information.

To reduce ethical risks, personal names, usernames, and sensitive organisational or technical details will be anonymized where appropriate. The collected PDF files and extracted information will be stored securely and deleted once the analysis is complete.

The data collection process was used to minimise impact on the targeted websites. The collection script checked each domain’s robots.txt file and avoided paths that were not permitted. It also used a random delay of 3.0–5.0 seconds between requests to reduce unnecessary load on servers. For transparency and replication, the scripts used for collection and analysis will be made available through GitHub. However, the collected PDF files and any sensitive extracted metadata will not be redistributed. Any processing of personal data will be considered in relation to GDPR Article 6, which states that processing is lawful only if at least one lawful basis applies \cite{eu-gdpr}.
